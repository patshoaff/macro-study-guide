Sessions 1–2 — GDP Fundamentals & Long-Run Growth

Why we measure economies · The production function · Growth accounting
Concept What is GDP and why do we evaluate countries' economies?
GDP is the total market value of all final goods and services produced within a country in a given period. We track GDP, inflation, and unemployment to gauge economic health, guide policy, and inform business/investment decisions.

GDP components (expenditure approach):

GDP = C + I + G + (X − M)
  • C (Consumption): ~68% of US GDP — household spending on goods/services
  • I (Investment): ~18% — business capital spending, residential construction, inventories
  • G (Government): ~17% — government purchases (NOT transfers like Social Security)
  • X − M (Net Exports): ~−3% — exports minus imports

Why all three matter together: GDP tells size of activity. Inflation tells whether prices are stable. Unemployment tells whether labor is being used. Together they paint the full picture for policymakers, investors, and firms.

Concept What are the 3 ways to increase GDP? (The Production Function)
Increase labor (↑L), increase capital (↑K), or increase productivity/technology (↑A). Productivity is the most important for sustained growth because L and K face diminishing returns.
Y = A × f(L, K)
FactorWhat it meansExamples
L (Labor)More workers / hoursImmigration, higher workforce participation, longer hours
K (Capital)More tools / machinesFactories, computers, infrastructure, equipment
A (TFP)Doing more with the sameInnovation, better management, education, technology adoption

Why A matters most: Adding more L and K has diminishing returns — the 1,000th worker adds less than the 10th. But productivity growth shifts the entire function upward, getting more output from the same inputs. This is the "perspiration vs. inspiration" distinction.

Example — Singapore (1965-92): Growth came almost entirely from ↑L (immigration, female participation) and ↑K (forced savings via CPF, FDI), with near-zero TFP growth. Critics argued this was unsustainable because it would hit diminishing returns — Singapore needed to shift toward innovation-driven growth.

Concept How might GDP or GDP/capita misrepresent individual experiences?
GDP per capita is an average that hides inequality, ignores non-market activity, and says nothing about quality of life, education, health, or environmental sustainability.
  • Income inequality: A country with $60K GDP/capita could have billionaires and poverty side by side. The average masks the distribution across groups, regions, races, and genders.
  • Non-market activity: Household work, caregiving, volunteer work — none counted.
  • Quality of life: Education, healthcare, life expectancy, happiness, leisure — all ignored.
  • Environmental damage: Depleting natural resources counts as "production" with no deduction.
  • Underground economy: Informal and illegal activity isn't captured.
  • Regional variation: National GDP masks huge differences (e.g., Mississippi vs. Connecticut).

Bottom line: GDP measures production, not prosperity or well-being.

Concept Global economic environment: Country vs. Firm perspective
Countries shape the economic environment (GDP growth, inflation, trade policy, institutions). Firms operate within it and respond to it (market access, labor costs, supply chains, regulatory burden).

Country level: Macro stability, fiscal/monetary policy, trade agreements, institutional quality, exchange rates. Countries set the "rules of the game."

Firm level: Market access, labor costs, supply chain logistics, taxation, political risk, consumer demand. Firms must navigate and adapt to the macro environment.

Key difference: A recession (macro) might push a firm to cut costs or exit a market. Strong GDP growth creates expansion opportunities. The macro environment is the playing field; firms are the players.

Concept Growth Accounting: How do we decompose where GDP growth came from?
Growth accounting breaks historical GDP growth into contributions from labor, capital, and productivity (TFP). It reveals whether growth is sustainable (productivity-driven) or hitting limits (input-driven).
Growth in Y = Growth in A + α(Growth in K) + (1−α)(Growth in L)

Why it matters: If a country's growth comes mainly from ↑L and ↑K (factor accumulation), it faces diminishing returns and will slow. If it comes from ↑A (productivity), it's more sustainable.

Example — Singapore: Growth accounting revealed near-zero TFP growth. The "miracle" was actually just massive input accumulation. This meant Singapore needed to pivot toward innovation, R&D, and moving up the value chain for growth to continue.

Example — France: High productivity per hour worked, but lower total GDP than the US because French workers work fewer hours (less L). Different countries prioritize differently.

Sessions 3–4 — AD-AS Model & Fiscal Policy

Business cycles · Aggregate demand & supply · GDP gap · Multiplier
Diagram The AD-AS Framework: What each curve means and how they interact
AD slopes down (higher prices = less spending). SRAS slopes up (higher prices = more profit for firms). MRAS is vertical at potential GDP (Y*). Their intersection determines the economy's price level and output.
CurveWhat it representsWhy that shape
ADTotal spending (C+I+G+NX) at each price levelDownward: higher prices → less purchasing power, higher rates, less competitive exports
SRASTotal output firms will produce (sticky wages)Upward: higher prices + fixed wages = higher margins → firms produce more. Steeper near capacity.
MRASPotential GDP at full employmentVertical: long-run output depends on real factors (L, K, A), not price level
Concept What shifts AD vs. SRAS? (Know this cold for the exam)
"Does this change how much people WANT to spend?" → AD. "Does this change firms' costs or ability to produce?" → SRAS. "Does this change the economy's total capacity?" → MRAS.

AD Shifters (Demand Side):

  • Consumer confidence (↑ → AD right)
  • Government spending (↑G → AD right)
  • Tax cuts (more disposable income → ↑C → AD right)
  • Interest rate cuts (↓rates → ↑I → AD right)
  • Stimulus checks (direct ↑C → AD right)
  • Lockdowns → ↓C, ↓I → AD LEFT

SRAS Shifters (Supply Side):

  • Input/oil prices (↑ → SRAS left)
  • Supply chain disruptions (SRAS left)
  • Technology improvements (↑productivity → SRAS right)
  • Regulation burden (↑ → SRAS left)
  • Natural disasters (SRAS left)

Demand shock: Prices and output move in same direction. Supply shock: Prices and output move in opposite directions (stagflation if SRAS left).

Diagram COVID Pandemic: 3 phases on the AD-AS diagram
Phase 1 (lockdown): AD left → P↓ Y↓. Phase 2 (CARES Act): AD right past Y* → P↑ Y↑. Phase 3 (supply chains): SRAS left → P↑↑ Y↓.

Why Phase 1 ≠ Phase 3: Both reduced GDP, but Phase 1 was a demand shock (prices fell), while Phase 3 was a supply shock (prices rose). This distinction matters for policy — you can fix demand shortfalls with stimulus, but stimulating demand during a supply shock just creates more inflation.

Why Phase 2 caused inflation: The $2T stimulus was ~3x larger than the ~$670B needed (GDP gap / multiplier). Too much money chasing too few goods, especially combined with Phase 3 supply constraints.

Concept Business cycles: What are they and why do they occur?
Short-run fluctuations between expansion and contraction, caused by demand shocks, supply shocks, financial crises, and policy mistakes — amplified by sticky wages and prices that prevent instant adjustment.

Causes: Changes in consumer/business confidence (AD), oil shocks or pandemics (SRAS), credit crunches (AD via ↓I), policy errors (central banks tightening too fast or governments over-stimulating).

Why the price level matters: It's the economy's thermometer. Rising prices → economy running "hot" (above potential). Falling prices → economy running "cold" (below potential). Stable prices → near equilibrium.

Example — 2008: Easy credit → AD pushed right (bubble) → crash → AD shifted sharply left → recession. Primarily a demand-side crisis triggered by financial sector collapse.

Calc GDP Gap, Consumption Function, and the Multiplier — the key fiscal policy calculation
GDP Gap = Potential − Actual GDP. Required Stimulus = GDP Gap ÷ Multiplier. The multiplier works because each dollar of spending becomes someone else's income, who spends a fraction (MPC) of it, creating a chain reaction.
GDP Gap = Y* − Yactual
Multiplier = 1 / (1 − MPC)
Required Stimulus = GDP Gap / Multiplier

Consumption Function: C = C₀ + MPC × (Y − T). MPC is the fraction of each extra dollar that gets spent (not saved).

Worked example (COVID):

  • GDP fell from ~$21.6T to ~$20.6T → GDP Gap ≈ $1T
  • Given multiplier = 1.5
  • Required stimulus = $1T / 1.5 = ~$670B
  • Actual CARES Act = $2T → ~3x too large → overshot Y* → inflation

Why the multiplier works: You get $1,000 → spend $670 (MPC=0.67) → that's someone's income → they spend $449 → that's someone's income → they spend $301... Each round smaller, but total > original amount.

Concept Fiscal Policy: Expansionary vs. Contractionary — what tools and when?
Expansionary (recession): ↑G, ↓T, direct transfers → shift AD right. Contractionary (overheating): ↓G, ↑T → shift AD left. The risk: overshoot causes inflation, undershoot prolongs recession.
ExpansionaryContractionary

COVID lesson: CARES Act was expansionary fiscal policy. It worked at restoring output but was too large, causing inflation. Contrast with 2009's stimulus, which was too small and left a prolonged recovery.

Example — Sri Lanka: Unsustainable fiscal policy (large tax cuts reducing revenue + excessive borrowing + costly infrastructure) → eventual sovereign default. Shows the danger of irresponsible fiscal expansion.

Session 5 — GDP & Government: Monetary Policy

Money supply · Central bank tools · Conventional vs. unconventional policy
Concept What is the money supply and how is it measured?
The money supply is the total amount of money circulating in an economy, measured in increasingly broad aggregates: M0 → M1 → M2 → M3.

M0 (Monetary Base): Currency in circulation + bank reserves held at the central bank. This is what the central bank directly controls.

M1 (Narrow Money): M0 + demand deposits (checking accounts) + other liquid deposits. Money most readily available for spending.

M2: M1 + savings accounts + time deposits under $100K + retail money market funds.

Key insight: The "money multiplier" means M1 and M2 are much larger than M0, because banks lend out deposits, creating new money. When the Fed does QE, it expands M0 directly, hoping the multiplier transmits this into broader money supply growth.

Concept What is the goal of monetary policy? What are the tools?
Goal: Price stability (~2% inflation) + maximum employment. Tools: open market operations, discount rate, reserve requirements, and unconventional tools (QE, forward guidance) when rates hit zero.

Conventional Tools:

  • Open Market Operations: Buying/selling government securities. Buying → injects reserves → lowers rates. The primary daily tool.
  • Discount Rate: Rate charged to banks borrowing from the Fed. Acts as ceiling on short-term rates.
  • Reserve Requirements: Fraction of deposits banks must hold (reduced to 0% in March 2020).

Unconventional Tools (at the zero lower bound):

  • QE: Large-scale asset purchases to lower long-term rates when short-term rates can't go lower.
  • Forward Guidance: Communicating future intentions to shape expectations.
  • Negative Rates: Charging banks for excess reserves (used by BOJ, ECB).

Transmission: Central bank changes short-term rate → borrowing costs change → C & I affected → AD shifts → inflation and employment respond.

Example — Japan's Lost Decade: BOJ cut rates too slowly, prematurely raised them twice (1997, 2000), and was reluctant to adopt QE until deflation was entrenched. Lesson: monetary policy is necessary but not sufficient in a balance-sheet recession — also need fiscal stimulus and banking cleanup.

Concept When should you use QE vs. conventional rate cuts? (AD-AS framework)
Use conventional rate cuts first. QE only when rates hit zero AND the problem is demand deficiency. Never use QE for supply shocks or when inflation is already high.

Use rate cuts when: Rates are above zero and there's a demand shortfall. This is the standard playbook.

Use QE when: Rates are at zero (zero lower bound), the economy is below potential, and you need additional stimulus. QE is the "break glass in emergency" tool.

DON'T use QE when:

  • Rates still above zero: Conventional cuts are preferred — more predictable.
  • Supply shock: SRAS shifts left → stagflation. QE shifts AD right, which raises output but pushes prices even higher. Can't fix a supply problem with demand stimulus.
  • Inflation already high: More monetary easing would worsen inflation.

COVID example: In March 2020, Fed Funds Rate was ~1.5%. The Fed cut rates to 0% first (conventional), then launched QE. Correct sequencing.

Session 6 — GDP & Institutions: National Differences

Formal vs. informal institutions · Property rights · Culture & growth
Concept What shapes institutions (formal and informal)?
Formal institutions (laws, property rights) are shaped by political processes and can change quickly. Informal institutions (culture, norms, trust) evolve slowly through history, religion, and social learning.

Formal: Laws, regulations, constitutions, property rights, contract enforcement. Created by deliberate action; can be reformed relatively quickly.

Informal: Culture, social norms, trust, customs, traditions. Evolve over generations; much harder to change deliberately.

Key shapers:

  • Historical path dependence: Colonial origins, past political systems create lasting patterns.
  • Geography & resources: Natural endowments shape what institutions develop (extractive vs. inclusive).
  • Religion & philosophy: Influence attitudes toward work, savings, authority, trust.
  • Political power: Those in power shape formal institutions to protect their interests.

North & Thomas thesis: Institutions (especially property rights) are the fundamental cause of economic growth.

Concept How do property rights and culture impact economic growth? (Use Y = A·f(K,L))
Property rights drive K (investment — you invest when you keep returns). Culture shapes L (work ethic, education) and A (innovation, trust, efficiency). Both feed directly into the production function.

Property rights → K: Secure rights → willingness to invest → capital accumulation. Without them, no one builds long-term because assets can be seized. De Soto's "dead capital": in developing countries, people hold assets but can't use them as collateral without formal titles.

Culture → L and A: Work ethic, education attitudes, trust, and cooperation affect labor productivity. High-trust cultures have lower transaction costs → more efficient markets. Cultural openness to innovation affects technology adoption (A).

True or False — Culture is biologically inherited? FALSE. Culture is learned through socialization, not inherited biologically. But TRUE that culture is shared by group members — it's a collective phenomenon.

Cross-cultural sensitivity: Services and consumer-facing goods (food, entertainment, management practices) are most sensitive to cultural differences. Commodities and standardized industrial goods are least sensitive. Implication: the more culturally sensitive the product, the more a firm must localize.

Sessions 7–8 — Open Economy: Balance of Payments & Exchange Rates

BoP components · FX determinants · PPP · J-Curve · Pegs vs. floats
Concept How does the Balance of Payments work and affect GDP?
Net exports (X−M) are a direct GDP component. The BoP always balances: current account deficit = financial account surplus (borrowed foreign capital). Persistent deficits mean accumulating foreign debt.

Current Account + Capital Account + Financial Account = 0

A current account deficit (importing > exporting) must be offset by a financial account surplus (foreign investment flowing in). Example: US runs persistent deficits, financed by foreigners buying Treasuries.

Current account surplus → currency strengthens (foreigners need your currency to pay for your exports → more demand for it).

Policy implication: Neither surpluses nor deficits are inherently good/bad — depends on whether borrowed capital is invested productively.

Concept What determines exchange rates? (Interest rates, inflation, trade, capital flows)
Higher interest rates → attract capital → currency appreciates. Higher inflation → currency depreciates (PPP). Trade surplus → more demand for currency → appreciates. Expectations and speculation dominate short-run moves.
  • Interest Rate Parity: Higher domestic rates attract foreign capital → appreciation.
  • PPP: Long-run, rates adjust so identical goods cost the same across countries. Higher inflation → depreciation.
  • Trade flows: Surplus countries see currency appreciate (foreigners need their currency).
  • Capital flows: FDI and portfolio investment create demand for domestic currency.
  • Expectations: Markets are forward-looking — expected changes move rates today.

Key relationships to memorize:

  • ↑Interest rates → currency STRENGTHENS
  • ↓Interest rates → currency WEAKENS
  • Current account surplus → currency STRENGTHENS
  • Current account deficit → currency WEAKENS
Concept How do exchange rates affect the BoP? (J-Curve)
Weaker currency → exports cheaper, imports pricier → trade balance improves over time. But short-run it worsens (J-Curve) because existing contracts are still in old currency terms.

Depreciation → BoP improvement (eventually): Exports become cheaper for foreigners → volume rises. Imports become more expensive → volume falls.

J-Curve: In the short run, depreciation can worsen the trade balance because existing import contracts cost more at the new rate. Over 6-18 months, volumes adjust and the balance improves — tracing a "J" shape.

Self-correcting mechanism: A trade deficit puts downward pressure on currency → cheaper exports → deficit eventually corrects (if floating rate).

Concept Why do countries peg exchange rates? What's the tradeoff?
Pegs reduce FX volatility, anchor inflation, and promote trade stability. The tradeoff (Impossible Trinity): you give up independent monetary policy.
  • Reduce uncertainty: Fixed rates eliminate FX risk for traders/investors.
  • Inflation anchor: Pegging to USD imports the Fed's monetary discipline.
  • Attract investment: Predictable exchange rates reduce investor risk.

Impossible Trinity: A country CANNOT simultaneously have: (1) fixed exchange rate, (2) free capital flows, AND (3) independent monetary policy. Pick two. By pegging, you sacrifice independent monetary policy.

Calc PPP & Big Mac Index: How to calculate implied rate and under/overvaluation
Implied PPP rate = Foreign price ÷ US price. Compare to spot rate. If spot rate shows more foreign currency per dollar than PPP implies, the foreign currency is undervalued.

Example: Big Mac costs 75 pesos in Mexico, $5.25 in US. Spot rate = 18 MXN/USD.

PPP rate = 75 / 5.25 = 14.29 pesos/USD

Spot rate = 18 pesos/USD (you need MORE pesos per dollar than PPP suggests)

→ Peso is undervalued by (14.29−18)/18 = −20.6%

Why spot ≠ PPP: Transportation costs, tariffs, non-traded goods, capital flows, speculation, government intervention. PPP is a long-run anchor; short-run rates are driven by capital flows.

Sessions 9–10 — International Trade

Comparative advantage · PPFs · Gains from trade · Economies of scale · Tariffs
Concept Comparative Advantage: What is it and how do you find it from a PPF?
A country has comparative advantage in the good it can produce at a lower opportunity cost. Find it by comparing the slope of each nation's PPF — the country that gives up LESS of the other good per unit has the advantage.

Absolute advantage: Who can produce MORE of a good (total output). Comparative advantage: Who can produce it at a LOWER OPPORTUNITY COST (giving up less of the other good).

How to find it from a PPF diagram:

  • Look at each country's PPF endpoints. For example: Country A can make 3 coffee or 3 sugar. Country B can make 1 coffee or 3 sugar.
  • Opportunity cost of 1 sugar for A: Give up 3/3 = 1 coffee
  • Opportunity cost of 1 sugar for B: Give up 1/3 coffee = 0.33 coffee
  • B gives up LESS coffee per sugar → B has comparative advantage in sugar
  • A gives up less sugar per coffee (1 sugar vs. 3 sugar for B) → A has comparative advantage in coffee

Key insight: Even if one country is better at EVERYTHING (absolute advantage), both countries still gain from trade by specializing in their comparative advantage. This is Ricardo's central insight.

Concept Gains from Trade: Who benefits most and why?
Partners with the MOST different opportunity costs gain the most from trade. The more different your PPF slopes are, the bigger the gains from specialization and exchange.

Why more difference = more gains: If two countries have identical PPFs (same opportunity costs), there's nothing to gain from trade — they'd just swap identical goods. The more their opportunity costs differ, the more each can specialize in what they're relatively good at and trade for the rest.

Example from the quiz: Countries A&B have somewhat similar PPFs. Countries C&D have very different PPFs (C is great at coffee but terrible at sugar; D is great at sugar but terrible at coffee). C&D gain MORE from trade because their opportunity costs are more different — specialization creates bigger total output gains.

Complete specialization: Under full specialization based on comparative advantage, each country produces ONLY the good they have comparative advantage in, then trades. Total combined output is maximized.

Watch out for tricky True/False: Having absolute advantage in a good ≠ comparative advantage. A country can be worse at everything in absolute terms and still gain from trade through comparative advantage.
Concept Economies of Scale: What are they and what are they NOT?
Economies of scale = producing more lowers average cost per unit. They result from specialization and comparative advantage. They do NOT increase average costs — that's the opposite (diseconomies of scale).

What economies of scale ARE:

  • When more is produced, average cost per unit decreases
  • Partially the result of specialization (workers get better at specific tasks)
  • Partially the result of comparative advantage (countries focus on what they're best at)
  • Spreading fixed costs over more units

What economies of scale are NOT:

  • They do NOT lead to increased average costs — that's the definition of diseconomies of scale (the opposite)

Connection to trade: Trade enables economies of scale because countries can produce for a larger market. Instead of producing small quantities of everything, a country specializes and produces large quantities of fewer goods, driving down per-unit costs.

Diagram Tariffs vs. Free Trade: The trade diagram (know this cold)
No trade: domestic S&D cross at P_A, Q_A (high price, low quantity). Free trade: foreign supply lowers price to P_B, quantity rises to Q_B. Tariff: price rises back toward P_A, quantity falls — consumers lose, domestic suppliers win.

Key relationships:

  • Free trade → lower prices, higher quantity. Consumers win; domestic producers face more competition.
  • Tariff → price increases, quantity decreases (compared to free trade). Domestic suppliers gain protection; consumers pay more; foreign suppliers lose market access.
  • Who is better off with tariffs (vs. free trade)? Domestic suppliers only.
  • Who is better off with free trade (vs. tariffs)? Domestic consumers AND foreign suppliers.
Concept Arguments for and against free trade vs. protectionism
Free trade maximizes total output via comparative advantage and lowers prices. Protectionism shields domestic industries but raises consumer prices and can invite retaliation. Most economists favor free trade with targeted support for displaced workers.

For free trade:

  • Comparative advantage → higher total output for all trading partners
  • Lower prices for consumers
  • Greater variety of goods available
  • Economies of scale from larger markets
  • Competitive pressure drives innovation

For protectionism (tariffs, quotas):

  • Infant industry: Protect new domestic industries until they're competitive
  • National security: Maintain domestic production of critical goods (defense, semiconductors)
  • Job protection: Shield domestic workers from foreign competition (though often temporary)
  • Unfair trade practices: Counter dumping or foreign subsidies

The nuance: Free trade creates winners (consumers, efficient producers) and losers (workers in uncompetitive industries). The gains outweigh the losses in aggregate, but the losses are concentrated while gains are diffuse — creating political pressure for protection.

Calc How to calculate opportunity cost from a PPF (step-by-step)
Opportunity cost of Good X = (Max of Good Y you give up) ÷ (Max of Good X you can make). The country with the LOWER opportunity cost for a good has comparative advantage in it.

Step-by-step method:

Given a linear PPF with endpoints: Max Coffee on Y-axis, Max Sugar on X-axis.

Opportunity cost of 1 Sugar = Max Coffee ÷ Max Sugar
Opportunity cost of 1 Coffee = Max Sugar ÷ Max Coffee

Worked example:

CountryMax CoffeeMax SugarOC of 1 SugarOC of 1 Coffee
A333/3 = 1 coffee3/3 = 1 sugar
B131/3 = 0.33 coffee3/1 = 3 sugar

B has lower OC for sugar (0.33 < 1) → B has comparative advantage in sugar

A has lower OC for coffee (1 < 3) → A has comparative advantage in coffee

Under complete specialization: A makes 3 coffee, B makes 3 sugar. Total = 3 + 3 = 6 units. Without specialization, total would be less.

Final Review — Cross-Cutting Practice Questions

Questions that test connections across all units
Quiz A hurricane destroys 30% of oil refineries. Which curve shifts? What happens to P and Y?
SRAS shifts LEFT. Prices ↑, Output ↓ (stagflation). This is a supply shock — higher production costs with less capacity.

Oil is a key input. Destroyed refining capacity raises costs for virtually all production. Firms produce less at higher prices. This is the worst type of shock because traditional demand-side policy can't fix it without causing more inflation.

Calc Potential GDP = $10T, Actual = $9.2T, Multiplier = 2.0. How much stimulus?
$400 billion. GDP Gap = $800B. Required stimulus = $800B / 2.0 = $400B.

Each $1 of stimulus creates $2 of GDP. So $400B generates $800B of total GDP impact, exactly closing the gap. If the government spent more, AD would overshoot Y* → inflation.

Quiz A country lowers interest rates. What happens to its currency and trade balance?
Currency WEAKENS (capital flows out seeking higher returns). Trade balance IMPROVES over time (weaker currency → cheaper exports, pricier imports) — but worsens first (J-Curve).

Lower rates → less attractive to foreign investors → sell domestic currency → depreciation. Weaker currency makes exports cheaper → export volume rises, import volume falls → current account improves after J-Curve adjustment period of 6-18 months.

Quiz Country X can make 10 cars or 5 computers. Country Y can make 4 cars or 8 computers. Who has comparative advantage in what?
X has comparative advantage in CARS (OC = 0.5 computers per car vs. Y's 2). Y has comparative advantage in COMPUTERS (OC = 0.5 cars per computer vs. X's 2).

X: OC of 1 car = 5/10 = 0.5 computers. Y: OC of 1 car = 8/4 = 2 computers. X gives up less → X has CA in cars.

X: OC of 1 computer = 10/5 = 2 cars. Y: OC of 1 computer = 4/8 = 0.5 cars. Y gives up less → Y has CA in computers.

Under complete specialization: X makes 10 cars, Y makes 8 computers. Total = 18 goods vs. without trade each making some of both.

Quiz A country imposes tariffs. Who wins, who loses, and what happens to price/quantity?
Price increases, quantity decreases. Domestic producers win (protected from competition). Domestic consumers and foreign suppliers lose (higher prices, less market access).

Tariffs act as a tax on imports, raising the effective price of foreign goods. This lets domestic producers charge more and sell more, but consumers pay higher prices for fewer goods. Net welfare loss for the economy overall — the gains to producers are smaller than the losses to consumers (deadweight loss).

Quiz How do institutions connect to the production function? (Essay-style)
Institutions affect ALL parts of Y = A·f(K,L). Property rights → K (investment). Culture/education → L (human capital). Rule of law, contract enforcement → A (efficiency). Institutions are the fundamental cause of growth differences across countries.

Strong institutions create the conditions for growth: people invest (↑K) when they know they'll keep returns, they invest in education (↑L quality) when there are economic opportunities, and good governance ensures resources are allocated efficiently (↑A). This is the North & Thomas argument and connects directly to why Singapore's government policies, for all the lack of TFP growth, at least built the institutional foundation for later productivity improvements.
